## Abstract

Generative AI tools have fundamentally changed what students can produce and how quickly they can produce it. Most frameworks developed to guide educators through this shift were designed for conversational AI — the ask-and-answer model — and do not fully account for agentic AI systems capable of planning, using tools, searching the web, and executing complex multi-step tasks with minimal human input. This paper introduces the Agency Spectrum, a practical framework for educators, school leaders, curriculum designers, and policymakers navigating this new terrain. The framework identifies three distinct roles a learner can occupy when working with agentic AI — Author, Designer, and Conductor — and argues that the central pedagogical question is no longer how much AI a student uses, but who is steering. It also introduces five constants that apply across all three roles: the non-negotiable responsibilities that remain with the learner regardless of how capable AI systems become.

---

## The Problem

For the past several years, educators have been grappling with a version of this question: how much AI involvement in student work is appropriate? Frameworks built around that question — including Jane Beckwith's AI Friction Scale (2025) — have been enormously useful. They gave educators a shared language for discussing productive struggle, cognitive demand, and learning integrity in a world where AI could draft, summarize, and explain on demand.

That question is not obsolete. But it is no longer sufficient.

Agentic AI changes the landscape in a specific and important way. Where conversational AI responds to a question, agentic AI pursues a goal. A student who types a prompt and receives a response is operating in a fundamentally different environment than a student who gives an AI agent a task and returns ten minutes later to review completed work. In the second scenario, the AI has already made decisions: what to research, what sources to trust, what argument to construct, what to include and exclude. The student's role is not to work alongside AI but to evaluate and accept or reject what AI has already done.

When that shift goes unexamined, the consequences are real. Students may produce sophisticated work they cannot explain or defend. They may lose the opportunity to develop the skills that the task was designed to build. They may conflate the ability to prompt an AI with the ability to think, write, or reason. And teachers, without a framework for evaluating what a student actually contributed to a piece of work, may find themselves assessing the AI's capabilities rather than the student's learning.

The Agency Spectrum is a response to that gap.

## The Framework

The Agency Spectrum identifies three roles a learner can occupy when working with AI in an academic or professional context. These roles are not a hierarchy. None is inherently better or worse than the others. Each serves legitimate learning purposes in the right context, and each creates distinct risks when used in the wrong one. The critical skill — for both educators and learners — is knowing which role is appropriate for a given task and why.

### The Author

*The student does the work. AI assists at moments.*

In the Author role, the learner owns the execution. They write the draft, conduct the research, develop the argument, and make the decisions. AI may appear at the margins — a student asks what a word means, requests grammar feedback on a finished draft, or uses AI to check a citation. But the substantive cognitive work is theirs.

This is the mode that existing frameworks were built for. The AI Friction Scale's five levels — from no AI through full AI assistance — describe variations within the Author role. They capture the difference between a student who writes without any AI involvement and a student who leans on AI for brainstorming, structure, or feedback while still doing the writing themselves.

The skills the Author role develops are foundational: writing fluency, research process, argumentation, persistence, close reading, and the capacity to sustain a line of reasoning over time. The skills it does not develop are equally worth noting: delegation, AI evaluation, and the ability to define a problem with enough precision that someone (or something) else can execute it well.

A 10th grader working on a history essay in Author mode might ask an AI what "entente" means, get her answer, and return to writing. Twenty minutes later, stuck on a paragraph, she reworks her thesis twice before it clicks. Her inner voice is something like: *This is hard, but I think my argument is getting stronger.* That difficulty is the learning.

The teacher's role in this mode: design the task, coach during execution, and assess both the product and the process.

### The Designer

*The student defines the problem and delegates execution.*

In the Designer role, the learner's work happens at the front end of the task. They define the problem, set the constraints, specify the direction, and then hand execution to AI. Their ongoing contribution is critical evaluation: reviewing what AI produces, identifying what is missing or weak, and giving specific, actionable feedback to improve it.

This mode requires a different kind of expertise than the Author role. A student cannot delegate effectively to AI without knowing enough about the subject to recognize a weak argument, an irrelevant source, or a tone mismatch. The Designer role develops problem definition, critical evaluation, editorial judgment, and communication clarity. It does not develop writing fluency, research process, or sustained drafting.

The same 10th grader, working in Designer mode on the same history essay, opens an AI tool and types: "I want to argue that the alliance system was the primary cause of WWI. Use only sources from 1910 to 1914. Write a formal, argumentative essay, not descriptive." She reviews the draft. The second paragraph is weak. She sends it back: "Add the naval arms race as a concrete example here." Her inner voice is: *I know what I want this to say. I need to be more specific about what's missing.*

She has not practiced writing. She has practiced something else: the capacity to hold a clear intellectual vision and communicate it with enough precision that AI can execute against it. That is a real and valuable skill. It is also not the skill the assignment was designed to build.

The teacher's role in this mode: design constraints that force meaningful direction-setting, and assess the quality of the student's problem framing, not just the output.

### The Conductor

*The student conducts an orchestra of AI agents toward a goal.*

In the Conductor role, the learner sets a goal and AI executes the full pipeline to reach it — researching, synthesizing, drafting, and self-reviewing with minimal student involvement in individual steps. The student's contribution is oversight: monitoring the process, catching errors, recognizing when AI has made the wrong call, and deciding what is acceptable and what needs to be revised or rejected.

This is largely new territory for K-12 education. The Conductor role is not a more extreme version of the Designer role — it is qualitatively different. The Designer directs; the Conductor supervises. The Designer says "write this with these constraints"; the Conductor says "achieve this goal" and lets AI determine the path.

The skills the Conductor role develops are real and increasingly important: pattern recognition, quality monitoring, bias detection, and verification. The skills it does not develop are just as real: writing, research, argumentation, and deep content engagement. A student who consistently operates in Conductor mode may develop sophisticated judgment about AI outputs while losing — or never building — the foundational capabilities that make that judgment meaningful.

The same student, working in Conductor mode, tells an AI agent: "Write a strong analytical essay on WWI causes for my history class. Find good primary sources. Make it argumentative, not descriptive." She checks back ten minutes later, skims the draft, notices it reads more like a summary, and flags it. She also catches a source from 1920 and rejects it as too late. Her inner voice is: *Something's off with the tone. And that source is too late.*

She has exercised real judgment. She has not practiced writing, research, or argumentation in any meaningful sense.

The teacher's role in this mode: design checkpoints and accountability structures, and teach students how to verify, question, and take ownership of work they did not execute themselves.

## What You Never Outsource

The three roles describe where a learner sits on the spectrum. They do not, by themselves, describe how to occupy any of those positions responsibly. A student can be an Author who never questions what AI tells them. A student can be a Designer who obscures how much AI contributed to their work. A student can be a Conductor who accepts AI outputs uncritically and submits them as their own judgment.

The five constants below apply across all three roles. They are not a checklist. They are dispositions — the commitments that remain with the learner regardless of which mode they are operating in, and regardless of how capable AI systems become.

**Direct.** You decide whether AI belongs in this work, and where on the spectrum you stand. You do not default to the most convenient mode. That decision is always yours.

**Question.** You treat every output as a draft, not a verdict. Confidence is not accuracy. Fluency is not correctness. These tools are designed to agree with you. Your skepticism is the counterweight.

**Own.** You know which parts of the work are genuinely yours. You can say so clearly. You do not obscure the line between what you built and what AI produced.

**Consider.** You think past the artifact. What are you not learning by not doing this yourself? Who is affected when this work enters the world? What assumptions came embedded in the model?

**Grow.** You are not finding your role and staying there. Your fluency across all three modes develops over time. Moving between Author, Designer, and Conductor with judgment is the actual goal.

These five constants reflect something that no AI system can supply: the deliberate human presence that makes AI use honest, purposeful, and educationally sound. They apply equally to students, teachers, and the professionals and institutions that shape how AI enters classrooms and organizations.

## Implications

### For Educators

The most immediate application of this framework is in task design. Before assigning work in an environment where agentic AI is accessible, the relevant question is: which role do I want students to occupy, and why? A research essay in which students are expected to develop writing fluency is not well served by Designer or Conductor modes, even if those modes produce more polished outputs. A capstone project that genuinely requires students to define a complex problem and direct resources toward it might be exactly the right context for Designer-mode work.

Assessment also changes. A teacher evaluating a piece of student writing produced in Conductor mode is not evaluating the student's writing. They may be evaluating the student's judgment, their ability to recognize quality, their capacity to catch errors — but not their ability to write. Both things may be worth assessing. They are not the same thing.

The framework does not prescribe which mode students should use. It asks teachers to be intentional about the choice and honest about what each mode does and does not develop.

### For School Leaders and Policymakers

Most current AI policies in educational institutions are written for conversational AI. They address plagiarism, disclosure, and appropriate use at the level of the prompt-and-response interaction. Agentic AI requires a different policy frame — one that accounts for the fact that AI can now do substantial intellectual work before a student ever reviews it.

The Agency Spectrum offers a vocabulary for that policy work. Rather than prohibiting or permitting AI in categorical terms, institutions can ask: what modes of AI engagement are appropriate for different types of work, at different stages of a student's development, in different disciplines? That question produces more durable and more educationally coherent policy than a binary allow/prohibit framing.

Equity is also a central consideration. Access to capable agentic AI tools is not evenly distributed. Students in well-resourced schools and households encounter these tools earlier and with more support. If the skills that agentic AI develops — problem framing, delegation, quality monitoring, verification — become the skills that matter most in the workforce and in higher education, then the gap between students who develop those skills with scaffolding and students who develop them without it, or not at all, is a gap that compounds over time.

### For Curriculum Designers

The framework suggests a developmental arc for AI integration that mirrors the developmental arc of learning more broadly. Younger learners and novice students in any domain are generally better served by Author-mode work, where foundational skills are built through execution. As expertise develops, Designer-mode work becomes more appropriate — students have enough domain knowledge to direct AI meaningfully. Conductor-mode work, with its demands for sophisticated oversight and verification, is appropriate for learners with genuine expertise in the subject.

This is not a strict hierarchy. Context matters. A student with no chemistry background might appropriately use Designer mode to explore a concept, while a student with strong writing fluency might use Conductor mode as a time-efficient path through an assignment that is not central to their learning goals. But the developmental logic — Author first, then Designer, then Conductor, in proportion to growing expertise — provides a useful default.

### For Ed-Tech Designers and AI Developers

The framework has implications for how AI tools are built and how they communicate with users. A tool that makes the Author/Designer/Conductor distinction visible — that shows users which mode they are operating in and what they are and are not developing — would be more educationally valuable than one that simply maximizes the quality of its output. A tool that surfaces the five constants as reflective prompts at the end of an interaction would support the habits of mind that make AI use responsible.

The design of AI tools shapes the dispositions of the people who use them. Tools optimized for speed and agreement produce users who prioritize speed and agreement. Tools designed with the Agency Spectrum's commitments in mind would produce something different: users who are intentional, skeptical, transparent, thoughtful, and growing.

## A Note on the Broader Field

The Agency Spectrum builds on and extends the AI Friction Scale developed by Jane Beckwith (2025), which provides a powerful framework for understanding levels of AI involvement in student work within the conversational AI paradigm. The three-role model and the walkthrough methodology described in this paper are original contributions designed to extend that thinking into the agentic era.

The framework is also in conversation with a broader body of emerging work on GenAI literacy, cognitive offloading, and student agency in the age of capable AI systems — including research from Anthropic (Shen and Tamkin, 2026), the Brookings Institution (Burns et al., 2026), the OECD (2026), and UNESCO (2024). The Agency Spectrum does not claim to resolve the questions this field is wrestling with. It claims to offer educators a practical, durable, and honest vocabulary for making better decisions in classrooms today.

---

## Conclusion

The arrival of agentic AI in educational settings is not a future event. It is a present one. Students are already using these tools. The question for educators, schools, and policymakers is not whether to engage with this shift but how to do so in ways that protect what learning is for.

The Agency Spectrum begins with a simple premise: when AI can do the work, the most important decision is who is steering. The three roles — Author, Designer, Conductor — give educators and learners a shared language for making that decision consciously. The five constants — Direct, Question, Own, Consider, Grow — describe what it means to steer responsibly.

These ideas are not a final answer. They are a starting point for the conversations that need to happen in every school, every district, every organization navigating this moment. The framework will evolve as the technology evolves and as practitioners test these ideas in real classrooms with real students.

Feedback, critique, and collaboration are welcome.
